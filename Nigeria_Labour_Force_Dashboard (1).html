Unemployment rate by education level
Post-secondary graduates face the highest unemployment of any group — 9.0%
Key finding: More education raises unemployment risk — until postgraduate level. A degree is not a job guarantee in Nigeria.
Unemployment trend
Brief recovery through 2023 fully reversed by Q1 2024 — back to Q4 2022 levels
Trend: The improvement seen in Q1–Q2 2023 was short-lived. The labour market is essentially stagnant.
The gender triple disadvantage
Women face higher unemployment, less wage work, and greater informality simultaneously
Male Female
Gender gap: Female unemployment (6.2%) is 44% higher than male (4.3%). Women are also 10× less likely to hold formal wage employment.
The urban trap
Contrary to expectations, cities have higher unemployment and NEET rates than rural areas
Urban Rural
Urban paradox: Cities attract job-seekers who then can't find work. Urban NEET (16.6%) is 52% higher than rural (10.9%).
Unemployment by age group
Youth (15–24) are most vulnerable; risk declines steadily with age
Education vs informality
Only postgraduates escape informal work in meaningful numbers
Employment quality breakdown
Of Nigeria's 73.2% "employed" population — most hold precarious informal jobs
Informal 92.7% Formal 7.3%
73.2% are "employed" — but 9 in 10 have no formal contract or protection.
