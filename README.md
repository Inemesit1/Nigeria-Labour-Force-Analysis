# The Education Paradox: Does Going to School Guarantee a Job in Nigeria?
### An Investigative Data Analysis of Nigeria's Labour Market — NBS Q1 2024

---

## Overview
Nigeria's official unemployment rate of 5.3% sounds manageable. But when you 
look past the headline figure using NBS Labour Force Survey data, a more 
troubling picture emerges — one that falls hardest on the educated, the young, 
women, and city dwellers.

---

## The Problem
I wanted to answer one question:

> *Who is unemployment actually hitting hardest in Nigeria, and is education 
the solution most people assume it is?*

---

## Data Source
- **Source:** National Bureau of Statistics (NBS) Nigeria
- **Report:** Nigeria Labour Force Survey (NLFS) Q1 2024
- **Coverage:** 35,520 households across all 36 states + FCT
- **Period:** Q4 2022 – Q1 2024
- **Link:** [nigerianstat.gov.ng](https://www.nigerianstat.gov.ng)

## Key Findings

### 1. The Real Unemployment Rate is Nearly 3× the Official Figure
The headline unemployment rate is 5.3%. But once underemployment is included,
**15.3%** of Nigeria's labour force is underutilised. The official number 
significantly understates the scale of the problem.

### 2. Education Increases Unemployment Risk
| Education Level | Unemployment Rate |
|---|---|
| No Education | 2.7% |
| Primary | 4.0% |
| Secondary | 6.9% |
| Post-Secondary | **9.0%** ← highest |
| Post-Graduate | 2.0% |

More education raises unemployment risk — until postgraduate level. 
A degree is not a job guarantee in Nigeria.

### 3. Being Employed Doesn't Mean Much
**92.7%** of Nigeria's employed population work informally — no contracts, 
no benefits, no protections. Even among post-secondary graduates, 70.7% 
are trapped in informal work.

### 4. Women Face a Triple Disadvantage
- Female unemployment (**6.2%**) is 44% higher than male (4.3%)
- Women less likely to hold wage jobs (12.1% vs 20.1%)
- Women more concentrated in informal work (96.4% vs 88.7%)

### 5. The Urban Trap
Cities are not delivering on their promise:
- Urban unemployment (**6.0%**) is higher than rural (4.3%)
- Urban youth NEET rate (**16.6%**) is 52% higher than rural (10.9%)
- Cities attract job-seekers who then cannot find work

---

## Recommendation
Policy interventions focused purely on increasing education enrollment 
miss the point. The data suggests Nigeria needs structural reforms that 
create formal employment pathways — especially for urban youth and women 
with post-secondary qualifications who are currently the most unemployed 
and most likely to be trapped in informal work.
